A little bit about me

Hi there, my name is William (or Will for short), but most people know me by the nickname I thought was my real name until kindergarten, BJ (short for Billy-Josiah). I've always had a passion for technology of some kind. What exactly that was has evolved throughout my life, but the overall concept of technology has always stayed the same. Starting in middle school, I became very interested in spaceflight and aerospace engineering. During my 7th grade year, I also became interested in programming, more specifically the Python programming language. I started with the basics, text based games and simple scripts. At that point that's really all it was. However, in the summer between my Freshman and Sophomore years, I took some programming courses online, and started to learn much more advanced concepts. I learned how to use classes and objects, and in general more advanced concepts that assist in making code both cleaner and giving your programs more complex and advanced behavior. Over that summer I sharpened my skills with Python, and also picked up C, C++, C#, and PHP. Then, starting in my Sophomore year, I realized that this was really what I want to do with my life. I worked on several projects with Raspberry Pi, using a concept of Python, C++, and PHP (and with PHP of course comes HTML, CSS, and JavaScript). I created an access control system for the school, which I named Passage. It worked off of Raspberry Pi, and used a homegrown web interface and NFC (Near Field Communication) cards to allow students and staff to travel between buildings much more efficiently. I also built several other Raspberry Pi and Arduino projects, but to this day Passage is my proudest accomplishment. In the summer between Sophomore and Junior year, I took an online machine learning course, and that really opened my eyes to so many new possibilities. The course was tough, but I ended up completing it and earning the certificate. I loved machine learning, I was fascinated with it. I devoted a lot of time both in and out of school on learning more. I wanted to soak up all the knowledge I could. I started incorporating it into a lot of my projects, and I really enjoyed it. I really have a passion for machine learning, and I love to see all the amazing things it is capable of. My dream is to work with machine learning at Tesla. To this day I continue to innovate, and learn more advanced techniques, with the goal of my work eventually being used to power the self driving cars of the future.

TL;DR: I love computers, more specifically machine learning.

Rocket Engine Model

What do they really mean when they say "lets light this candle"?

Rocket Engine Model

However, during the course of the project my 3d printer stopped working and was unable to produce prints of the quality that I would require. That's when the goal shifted. Overall this project is a great example of a very vital idea in project based learning: you can't always build what you originally intend to, oftentimes you'll have to compromise and optimize the project to get done in a timely manner but still produce a good final product. 2018-19

NFC Door Locks

Access granted.

NFC Door Locks
To this day I would still consider this my biggest and proudest accomplishment. In terms of my high school career, this is where I transitioned from a focus of aerospace engineering to programming. The idea actually came about when I was curious how Apple Pay worked. Once I learned about the technology, I wanted to try it for myself. I came up with the idea of a cheap, easy to use access control system. I started off with a Raspberry Pi and a PN532 NFC reader. I created a PHP app that communicated with a MySQL database to store a list of all students and staff along with their card ID. Staff could login to the web interface and grant students passage to another building, which would allow for one round trip, exiting the origin building, entering and then exiting the destination, as well as entering back to the origin. There was also a flagging system where administrators could flag an ID, the system would warn but not stop access being granted to those cards. Additionally, there was an emergency lockdown system, which would put the system into a staff only mode. Lastly, it had an emergency mode for when internet connectivity was not possible. Administrators could use special access cards to lock the system into always open or always closed modes. Over the course of this project I learned some of the core skills needed to be successful as a developer: error troubleshooting and perserverance. There were many points where I got stuck on a really confusing bug, but I was always able to overcome it. I also learned how to wire components together and the various communications methods such as I2C and SPI, among others. In addition, I learned how to solder components together for the first time. Looking back on this project, knowing what I know now, I wish I woul've used a different stack for the app. I have grown to love Python's web servers and frameworks for back end processing, and frontend frameworks like React. The app would be more stable and more easily usable by the end user. Regardless, this is still one of those things I always look back on and feel a sense of pride.

SIR Model

Strangely relevant to 2021.

SIR Model
This project was one of my earlier ones to do with data science and visualization. I explored the SIR (Susceptible, Infected, Removed/Recovered) model that is used to model an infectious epedemic. The basic idea is that you start with a certain number of susceptible and infected people. Certain (usually statuc) parameters detail the rate in which susceptible become infected, and infected become recovered or removed. In specific, this project tried to model what could happen if the ebola virus outbreak of 2014 ever became widespread in Minneapolis. It used historical data to determine the rough casualty rate and the overall loss of population. In terms of programming, this was one of my first experiences with many popular data scient libraries like NumPy, Pandas, and Matplotlib. I gained experiences in how to effectively organize and present your data, as well as the basics of working with arrays and dataframes. I also put a big emphasis on code organization, making sure to document all functions and follow the PEP-8 style guide, the most widely endorsed style guide for writing python code.

2019-20

Natural Language Processing

Started as a joke, turned into a learning experience.

Natural Language Processing
This project was for one of our advisory's themed weeks. We had a week to create a project that fit the current theme. In this case, the theme was Shrek. I knew I wanted to somehow involve machine learning into this project, and at the time the technology that had me captivated was LSTM (Long Short Term Memory) networks. These kinds of networks can "memorize" sequences, making them very good at NLP (Natural Language Processing) among other things. Now how would this relate to Shrek? Well my ultimate goal was to analyze the entire script of the movie, and use a LSTM network to identify all of the puns within the script. This was by far the most ambitious machine learning project I had taken on as of then. Like any good project, it started with a lot of research into the logic behind a LSTM network, as well as techniques of natural language processing. The main challenge one faces is converting words that humans understand, to numbers, which is what algorithms understand. The typical technique is to use embedding vectors and word tokenizing. This basically can convert words into tokens, and then uses what's called an "embedding vector" to put words in a three dimensional space, where words with similar meanings are close to one another. Once I had done that, came the challenge of converting that data into the proper format a LSTM network requires. To do this, I had to experiment a lot with the NumPy module to reshape the input arrays into a way that, when fed into my network, would produce an accurate result. I would say the bulk of my project was spent on preprocessing the data in the most efficient manner. Finally, there was tuning the network's structure and parameters. After that, all that was left was to build a simple user interface, which I did using a module called PySimpleGUI, it isn't going to win any awards for design, but it gets the job done. It actually eneded up training to a rather high accuracy, and was very effective in the end. I learned a lot of very useful things for this project, and it would lay the groundwork for some of my other projects which also involved LSTM networks. I also got more aquainted with data preprocessing and working with NumPy.

Economics and ML

From puns to currency exchange.

Economics and ML
This project was built off of the previous project. Like the one before it, LSTM networks were the center of attention. Except instead of using the networks to understand language, it was going to understand currency exchange. Specifically, predicting the trends of exchange rates for the future given past data. At first, I was incredibly confused because the network was basically just making its predictions in a ramp shape. I spend weeks troubleshooting the issue, until I finally isolated the issue to a data processing issue. I had been formating the data returned from the network in the wrong way. After I fixed that issue, the network was creating very realistic predictions. While it would likely not work for a real world use because it can't take into consideration the political drivers that determine those. It can only try looking at previous trends and producing something that looks similar to it. Overall, this was one of those projects that really broadened my knowledge on machine learning but also taught me how to debug machine learning algorithms, something I wasn't very effective with.

Neural Attention

The devil is in the details.

Neural Attention
This project was also the result of one of the themed weeks we had in our advisory. For this week, the theme was the wild west. I was interested in exploring more advanced neural networks, so I picked neural attention. This is a model where the network can actually decide where to "focus" on the input to produce its output. Specifically, I wanted the network to be able to generate captions for images from the wild west and civil war areas of history. This project overall went well I think based on just the amount of learning I did. However, the actual outcome wasn't quite as impressive because the captions were very weak and couldn't really capture what was happening in the image. The main reason I attribute to this is just a limited amount of training data. It only had about 200 images, which is a small fraction of what you'd reasonably need to train something like this. Regardless, it was still able to use nerual attention to generate captions. If I was able to get more data to feed it, I think it would've behaved much better.

Evolutionary Learning

A NEAT way to view evolution.

Evolutionary Learning
This was one of my lasts projects I would do at NWPHS, so I wanted to do something impressive. I decided I wanted to play around with evolutionary learning. Specifically, I wanted to use NEAT (Neuro Evolution of Augmenting Topoligies), which is an algorithm that can simulate the process of evolution and natural selection. I wanted to earn science credit for this project, so I decided it would be an interesting and unique way to achieve those targets. Specifically, I wanted to use it to play the original Sonic the Hedgehog. Luckily, through the use of some awesome open source projects such as the OpenAI Gym and their Gym Retro project, I had a very easy to use premade interface between algorithm and the game. All that was left was to create the networks. Luckily, this was also fairly easy to do with a pre existing python module. For this project, most of the work came in the form of calculating the fitness of a partical run. It was difficult to come up with a method of calculating how well any given example did. Eventually, I was able to devise a method to give high scores high fitness, but also punish for genes that basically just "memorized" all the correct moves. While in the end, it could barely make it through the first half of the level, I still was proud it was even able to get that far.
